Programmatic Aesthetics and the TeX/LaTeX System: A Technical and Artistic SurveyPart 1: The Architecture of Programmatic Documents: From PostScript to the PDF Standard1.1 Introduction: Three Paradigms of Document GenerationThis report commences with a foundational analysis of programmatic document generation. To comprehend the aesthetic and technical specifics of the TeX/LaTeX system, one must first situate it within the broader historical landscape of digital document creation. All programmatic methods for generating fixed-layout documents can be classified into one of three competing philosophical paradigms: (1) The Page Description Language (PDL) model, which treats the document as a program to be executed; (2) The Document Format model, which treats the document as a static, stable container of display instructions; and (3) The Browser Engine model, which leverages web standards as a typesetting engine. The central narrative of this report—the Markdown -> Pandoc -> LaTeX workflow—operates in a complex dialogue with all three, simultaneously embracing and rejecting aspects of each.1.2 The First Paradigm: PostScript and the Procedural OriginalThe history of modern digital typesetting begins not with a static file format, but with a fully Turing-complete programming language: PostScript. Developed by Adobe's founders, John Warnock and Chuck Geschke, over 20 man-years, PostScript is a language for describing a page procedurally.1 When combined with Aldus PageMaker and the Apple LaserWriter, it ignited the "Desktop Publishing" revolution and became the undisputed "lingua franca of the prepress world".1Because PostScript is a language, a PostScript file is a program. This procedural nature makes it inherently "glitchable." A visual artifact in a PostScript document is not merely "corrupted data"; it is a bug in the program's execution, capable of producing complex, cascading, and often beautiful failures. This proceduralism was not always a bug; it could be a feature. As will be explored in Part 3, the legendary Beowolf font (1990) was an artistic exploit of this very quality, embedding randomizing PostScript code directly into the font file to alter its own outlines at print time.2 The industry's gradual phase-out of PostScript in favor of PDF 1 was, in effect, a deliberate move away from this volatile, generative model toward a declarative and stable one.1.3 The Second Paradigm: PDF, The "Camelot Project," and the Stable ContainerThe Portable Document Format (PDF) was conceived as a direct solution to the procedural volatility of PostScript and the "Babel" of incompatible document formats. In 1991, Adobe co-founder John Warnock initiated "The Camelot Project," which proposed a simplified, non-Turing-complete version of PostScript (then called Interchange PostScript, or IPS).3 The goal was to create a format that could be viewed and printed on any machine with perfect fidelity, ensuring the visual representation was identical everywhere.Adobe released the PDF 1.0 specification in 1993, initially as a proprietary format.3 Its adoption was slow until Adobe distributed its Adobe Reader (now Acrobat Reader) program free of charge, establishing PDF as the de facto standard for fixed-format electronic documents.5 The format evolved through numerous versions, adding features 4, until it was finally released as an open standard, ISO 32000-1, on July 1, 2008.3The entire philosophical purpose of the PDF is to prevent glitches and guarantee stability. It is a final-state container, not a live language. This shift from the procedural PostScript to the declarative PDF has profound implications for digital artists. To "glitch" a PDF, one can no longer easily "glitch the code," as the file is a static set of display instructions. Instead, the artist is forced to move their intervention upstream. They must attack the generator of the PDF (such as TeX or a browser engine) or the assets (such as fonts) before the stable, immutable PDF is ever created. This "upstream intervention" is the central premise of this report and the foundation of the modern programmatic art practices explored herein.1.4 The Third Paradigm: HTML-to-PDF and the Browser as RendererA third, parallel evolutionary path exists, one that bypasses the structured typesetting lineage of PostScript and TeX entirely: the rendering of standard web content (HTML, CSS, JavaScript) into a fixed PDF. This model is ubiquitous in modern business for "programmatic document generation" of invoices or reports 6, and it has also been adopted by a significant contingent of digital artists (see Part 5).This path has a notoriously troubled history. Early conversion methods were deeply flawed; developers in 2016 described the.NET PDF generation landscape as a "graveyard of abandoned projects and half-baked solutions".9 Early engines, such as those based on Internet Explorer's MSHTML component, were buggy and inconsistent.10The modern standard, for both business and creative use, involves using full-fledged headless browser engines, primarily Chromium.11 Tools like Playwright and WeasyPrint (discussed in Part 5) essentially "print to PDF" from a live, headless web browser, providing excellent fidelity for modern CSS3 and JavaScript-heavy layouts.12 High-fidelity commercial engines like PrinceXML are also used when typographic perfection is required.12 This "HTML-to-PDF" model represents the primary alternative to the TeX/LaTeX workflow. The artistic collectives in Part 5 who use html2print 14 are making an explicit philosophical choice for the web aesthetic (CSS, JS) over the TeX aesthetic (macros, boxes, glue). The Markdown -> Pandoc -> LaTeX workflow, which is the focus of this report, is a deliberate rejection of this web-native model, favoring a more stable, sustainable, plain-text-driven approach.15Part 2: The Central Narrative: TeX, LaTeX, and the Pandoc Toolchain2.1 The Knuthian Paradigm: Typesetting as AlgorithmWe now turn to the report's central narrative vehicle: the TeX typesetting system. Developed by programming legend Donald Knuth in 1978, TeX was an algorithmic response to the "abysmal" typographic quality of the digital typesetting systems of the era.16 Knuth's core philosophy was twofold: to create a system that could produce typeset output of the highest archival quality, and to empower authors without typographical expertise to generate these high-quality layouts themselves.17This philosophy led to the creation of TeX, a macro-based typesetting engine, and LaTeX, a "document preparation system" written in TeX macros that provides a more accessible, semantic layer for authors.16 This model, however, has a crucial and non-obvious consequence that is central to this report: it created a "split and separation of the roles of developer and designer".17 For many designers, the inner workings of TeX are "impenetrable".17 This separation is not a flaw; it is the central creative tension that programmatic artists and generative typographers exploit. They do not engage with TeX as a "designer" using a visual tool like PageMaker, nor merely as an "author" writing content. They engage with it as a "developer," programmatically intervening in the very macros and algorithms that Knuth separated from the content. The artistic practices detailed in Part 5 are, in effect, a deliberate effort to re-merge these segregated roles.2.2 Generative by Design: METAFONT, The Parametric OriginalIt is impossible to discuss TeX's generative potential without its companion system, METAFONT. Also developed by Knuth, METAFONT is not a font format, but a description language used to define vector fonts programmatically.19 A METAFONT file defines a glyph not by its static outline, but by a set of algebraic equations and parameters—such as stem width, x-height, and slant—that describe the "internal structure of the shapes".21Knuth's 1982 paper, "The Concept of a Meta-Font," explains this parametric idea.23 By changing the parameters, one can generate an entire family of fonts from a single source file. Knuth himself, with characteristic irony, queried the utility of this: "...does anybody really need a 6.5-point font that is one fourth of the way between Baskerville and Helvetica?".23The answer, from the perspective of generative art, is an emphatic yes. The artists and creative coders of today are the direct intellectual descendants of the METAFONT philosophy.25 METAFONT is the original generative typesetting tool, and its parametric, algebraic approach 21 is the conceptual precursor to all intentional glitch art in this domain. The "glitch toolkit" proposed in Part 4 of this report is, in essence, a reimplementation of the METAFONT philosophy, but applied to the entire document (layout, structure, text) rather than just the glyphs.2.3 The Modern TeX Engines: A Divergence of CapabilitiesTo understand TeX glitches, one must understand that "TeX" is not a single, monolithic program. It is a family of engines, and the evolution of these engines defines the creative possibilities available to an artist.27The original TeX engine (1978) declared "feature complete" by Knuth in 1989 27, output a Device Independent (DVI) file, which was typically converted to PostScript.29 Modern workflows, however, compile directly to PDF using one of three main "flavors" 28:pdfTeX: Written by Hàn Thế Thành in the late 1990s, pdfTeX was the first major extension of Knuth's original engine to output PDF natively.29 It is downward-compatible with original TeX but adds crucial new features, including "microtypography" (such as font protrusion and margin kerning) and primitives for PDF-native features.30XeTeX: Developed by Jonathan Kew, XeTeX's primary innovation was to merge modern font technologies and Unicode with TeX.29 For the first time, a TeX user could easily use any OpenType or TrueType font installed on their operating system without complex setup, and could input Unicode (e.g., non-Latin scripts) directly.31 For an artist, XeTeX opened the door to using the vast world of modern and experimental fonts.LuaTeX: This is the most significant development for programmatic art. LuaTeX is an extended version of pdfTeX that embeds the Lua scripting language directly into the typesetting engine.29The choice of engine is the artist's first creative decision. pdfTeX is the stable, classical instrument. XeTeX is the instrument for the font-obsessed. But LuaTeX is the generative instrument.32 It allows an artist to write and execute Lua code during the compilation process itself, hooking into the deepest internals of the typesetting algorithm. This provides a powerful, built-in mechanism for introducing programmatic randomization, conditional logic, and intentional, complex glitches, as will be detailed in Part 4.2.4 The Workflow as Medium: Markdown, Pandoc, and "Sustainable Authorship"This section addresses the specific Markdown -> Pandoc -> LaTeX toolchain, which is itself a medium and a form of conceptual practice. This workflow is articulated by the "Sustainable Authorship" philosophy: a practice centered on plain text (Markdown) that champions "platform independence" and a clean "separation of form and content".15The "glue" in this pipeline is Pandoc, a command-line "general markup converter" 35 that translates documents from one format to another (e.g., Markdown to LaTeX, HTML to.docx).15 Pandoc is the central engine for a wide range of academic and creative publishing workflows.18However, Pandoc is far more than a simple converter. Its workflow is "porous".17 Internally, Pandoc does not convert directly from Markdown to LaTeX. It first parses the Markdown into an intermediate, structured representation of the document known as an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST). It then walks this AST to "write" the output LaTeX file. This two-step process creates a powerful interception point. An artist can use Pandoc's filter system to run a script (e.g., in Lua) that intercepts and manipulates the document's structure after it has been parsed from Markdown but before it has been rendered into LaTeX. This is a far more subtle and powerful form of "glitching" than simple file corruption. One is not attacking the text, but the semantic structure of the document itself. This "porous" workflow is the explicit methodology used by the art collectives Varia 17 and, most significantly, by Christoph Haag in his etherpash project.35Part 3: A Taxonomy of Aesthetic Glitches in TeX/LaTeX (Aims A1, A2)3.1 Introduction to the TaxonomyThis section fulfills the objective of cataloguing and categorizing aesthetic glitches in the TeX/LaTeX ecosystem. "Glitch" is not a monolithic concept; it is a failure at a specific layer of the generation stack. A useful taxonomy must differentiate glitches based on their point of origin within the Input -> Pre-Processor -> Engine -> Post-Processor -> Viewer chain. These failures can be accidental (bugs) or intentional (artistic intervention).3.2 Category 1: Render-Level Glitches (The Viewer)These are the most ephemeral glitches, existing not in the PDF file itself but in the act of viewing it. They are artifacts of the PDF rendering software, not the TeX-generated data.Case Study: The TeXstudio / Qt6 BugA user on the TeX Stack Exchange forum reported a "weird visual glitch" that appeared only when zooming in on a PDF within the TeXstudio editor.38 Critically, the user noted the glitch did not occur when viewing the same file in the TeXworks editor.38 This proves the PDF file itself was valid. The technical cause was diagnosed as a "Qt6-bug"—a software bug in the Qt6 framework used by that specific version of TeXstudio—which was triggered by the user's non-standard "125% display scaling" setting.38Taxonomic Classification: This is a pure Render-Level Glitch. It is accidental and dependent on a specific software/hardware configuration. For an artist, this type of glitch challenges the core promise of the PDF: universal fidelity. The artwork becomes a "performance" that only exists on a specific system, severing the link between the data-object and the visual experience.3.3 Category 2: Pre-Processor Glitches (The Pandoc Layer)These glitches are intentionally introduced before the TeX engine is ever invoked, by manipulating the input data or the Pandoc converter itself.Case Study: Christoph Haag's "Markup Collage"As part of the etherpash workflow (detailed in Part 5), Christoph Haag developed a system that processes a "markup collage".35 The input is not clean Markdown, but an "ad hoc markup" with a "smell of source code," mixing plain text, Markdown, and custom shell commands.35Taxonomic Classification: This is an intentional Pre-Processor Glitch. The "glitch" is the emergent, unpredictable, and complex layout that results from forcing the Pandoc parser and Bash scripts 37 to interpret this hybrid, "broken" input. It exploits the "porous" 17 nature of the toolchain.3.4 Category 3: Engine-Level Glitches (The TeX Compilers)These are glitches that occur during the compilation process, caused by the TeX engine itself.Accidental (Incompatibility): The TeX engine family is not perfectly cross-compatible. pdfTeX introduced many new "primitives" (core commands).30 XeTeX and LuaTeX are based on the same core but do not implement all pdfTeX-specific primitives.30 An artist could attempt to use a pdfTeX-specific microtypography command in a XeTeX document. This would cause the engine to fail, producing either an error or, in some cases, an unpredictable (though likely unaesthetic) output.Intentional (Scripting): The LuaTeX engine is the primary vector for intentional engine-level glitches.29 An artist can use the \luaexec{...} command to inject arbitrary Lua code directly into the TeX compilation queue.33 For example, \luaexec{tex.print(math.random(1,100))} could be used to programmatically and randomly alter text, spacing, or even document structure during the typesetting process.Taxonomic Classification: This is a deep, intentional Engine-Level Glitch. The artist is intervening directly in the typesetting algorithm as it runs.3.5 Category 4: Macro-Level Glitches (The LaTeX Layer)These glitches are caused not by the engine (e.g., pdfTeX), but by the LaTeX macro packages (the "software" running on the engine).Case Study: Generative TikZTikZ is a popular and extraordinarily powerful LaTeX package for creating vector graphics.39 It is, in itself, a sophisticated programming language. Artists use it to create complex generative diagrams and "colorful, annotated equations".40 While this is programmatic art, a glitch would be the abuse of this system. For example, an artist could define a recursive TikZ macro that overloads the TeX memory, or, more subtly, use TikZ to draw graphics over the main text area, intentionally "glitching" and obscuring the document's content.Taxonomic Classification: This is an intentional Macro-Level Glitch. The artist is using a package's intended functionality for an unintended, destructive, or generative purpose.3.6 Category 5: Asset-Level and Post-Processor Glitches (Fonts)These are glitches where the TeX engine itself is working perfectly, but it is faithfully attempting to render a "broken" or programmatic asset (specifically, a font).Case Study 1 (Post-Processor): The Beowolf Font (1990)This legendary font by Erik van Blokland and Just van Rossum used a "randomizing algorithm".25 When used in a PostScript workflow (see Part 1.2), the font file itself contained PostScript code that would "generate different outlines every time" it was sent to the printer.2 This is a Post-Processor Glitch; the intervention happens after TeX has finished its job, inside the PostScript interpreter.Case Study 2 (Asset-Level): Antonio Roberts' Dataface (2012)The artist Antonio Roberts (aka Hellocatfood) wrote a program that intentionally corrupts font files, replacing "a certain portion of the font data by random values".41 This "databending" process results in a "glitch typeface."Taxonomic Classification: This is a pure Asset-Level Glitch. Based on the taxonomy developed in Part 3, the most powerful, flexible, and modern toolchain for artistic intervention is Markdown -> Pandoc (with Lua Filters) -> LuaLaTeX. This pipeline provides three distinct points of intervention: the pre-processor (Pandoc), the engine (LuaLaTeX), and the assets (fonts).4.2 Method 1: Semantic Glitching via Pandoc Lua FiltersThis is the most novel and structurally significant technique. Instead of creating mere visual noise, the artist attacks the semantic structure of the document.Blueprint: The toolkit would consist of a set of Pandoc Lua Filters. As established in Part 2.4, Pandoc can run a Lua script to traverse and modify the document's Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) mid-conversion. The toolkit would provide pre-made filters, such as:ast-scrambler.lua: A filter that walks the AST and randomly re-orders a percentage of the nodes. For example, it might find all Para (paragraph) blocks and Header (section title) blocks and randomly swap 10% of them, resulting in a structurally chaotic document.text-corruptor.lua: A filter that traverses the AST and applies a "databending" function (e.g., random character substitution, vowel removal) to all Str (string) elements, while leaving the structure (e.g., BulletList, CodeBlock) intact.type-shifter.lua: A filter that randomly changes the type of AST elements, for instance, turning 20% of BulletList nodes into OrderedList nodes, or CodeBlock nodes into BlockQuote nodes.This is a semantic-level glitch. The artist is not just creating visual noise; they are programmatically mutilating the meaning of the document before it ever reaches LaTeX. The result is a document that is both perfectly typeset by LaTeX (which happily processes the modified structure) and semantically shattered.4.3 Method 2: Typographic Glitching via LuaLaTeX HooksThis method operates inside the TeX engine, attacking the typesetting process itself using LuaTeX.29Blueprint: The toolkit would include a LaTeX class or package (e.g., glitchart.sty) that provides commands to inject Lua code at critical moments.Randomized Parameters: The package would use \luaexec to randomly alter TeX's core typesetting parameters (e.g., \hsize (horizontal width), \vsize (vertical height), \parindent (paragraph indentation)) at random intervals, causing the document's layout to visibly shift and break.Glyph-Level Intervention: The toolkit would use LuaTeX's advanced "callback" system to hook into the font rendering process. For example, a callback on pre_render_glyph could randomly replace, scale, or shift individual characters as they are being added to the page."Broken" Box-and-Glue: This is a more advanced technique. TeX's layout algorithm is based on a "box and glue" model. The toolkit would use Lua to programmatically interfere with this model, for example, by inserting random negative \kern (spacing) or \vss (vertical stretch) commands, forcing lines of text and even paragraphs to overlap, collapse, and overprint.4.4 Method 3: The Parametric "Glitch-Font" (The METAFONT Revival)This method provides a modern, intentional "glitch" revival of Knuth's original METAFONT philosophy.21Blueprint: The toolkit would include a tutorial and a template file (e.g., in METAFONT, MetaPost, or a modern parametric font editor) for creating a simple "unstable" font. For example, a parameter for "glyph-chaos" could be defined. When this parameter is set to 0, the letter 'A' renders normally. As the artist increases the "chaos" parameter, the script programmatically adds more random, extraneous points to the letter's outline, or randomly offsets its control points, resulting in an aesthetically broken, "glitched" glyph. This connects Knuth's original generative vision directly to a contemporary artistic aesthetic.Part 5: Survey of Programmatic Art & Hybrid Publishing (Aim B1)5.1 Introduction: From Glitch Art to a "FLOSS Design Practice"This section fulfills the objective of cataloguing artists and collectives using these programmatic systems.
